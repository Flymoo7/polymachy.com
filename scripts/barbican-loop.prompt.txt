A loose painterly fantasy oil painting comes to life in cinematic slow motion. Visible brushstrokes and canvas texture are preserved at all times; a hand-painted matte concept-art look, never photographic. The camera is completely locked: no pan, no zoom, no dolly.

In a castle barbican gateway, four armoured knights with their backs to the viewer brace to meet a charging enemy. The four knights move continuously and dynamically all the way through the shot, never freezing or holding still, yet they hold their ground and do not advance far forward. The grey-haired old knight on the left shifts his weight and readies his longsword. The young knight beside him hefts his poleaxe and leans into the fight. The female knight in the centre drops into a fighting stance, wielding a sword in each hand — two swords in total, one held in her right hand and one in her left hand. Both of her swords stay in her hands the entire time and never disappear, merge or change. A third sword is sheathed at her side and stays in its scabbard. On the far right the elf knight keeps his bow aimed straight ahead at the distant enemy horde and slowly draws the bowstring back; the nocked arrow stays on the bow and is never released, and he never turns toward his own companions.

Every weapon keeps exactly the same shape throughout and never transforms. Ahead of the knights, beyond the fallen soldier, the enemy horde charges hard and surges forward, many figures running toward the knights with weapons raised, full of churning motion. The castle walls, the gate and the sky stay still. Painterly throughout, the movement graceful, weighty and continuous.
