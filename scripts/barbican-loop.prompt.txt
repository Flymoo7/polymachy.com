A loose painterly fantasy oil painting drifts almost imperceptibly to life in very slow, gentle slow motion. Visible brushstrokes and canvas texture are preserved at all times; a hand-painted matte concept-art look, never photographic. The camera is completely locked: no pan, no zoom, no dolly.

In a castle barbican gateway, four armoured knights with their backs to the viewer hold their ground against a charging enemy. The four knights barely move and they do NOT step, stride, walk, run or advance forward — they do not travel across the ground at all. They stay rooted exactly where they stand, shifting only a little and very slowly: a small slow shift of weight, a slight turn of the shoulders or head, the soft sway of cloaks, hair and weapon tips. The grey-haired old knight on the left holds his longsword raised and extended up toward the enemy, and he keeps that sword raised high the entire time, never lowering it — only a small slow shift of weight. The young knight beside him slowly hefts his poleaxe a fraction. The female knight in the centre slowly settles into her stance, keeping a sword in each hand — two swords in total, one in her right hand and one in her left — both staying in her hands the whole time, never disappearing, merging or changing; a third sword is sheathed at her side and stays in its scabbard. On the far right the elf knight aims his bow forward at the distant charging horde near the gate ahead, the nocked arrow pointing toward the far-off enemy, and he very slowly draws the bowstring back a little; the arrow stays on the bow and is never released, and the bow never swings toward the other knights standing beside him.

Every weapon keeps exactly the same shape throughout and never transforms. Ahead of the knights, beyond the fallen soldier, the distant enemy horde presses forward, figures moving toward the knights. The castle walls, the gate and the sky stay still. All motion is slow, small, gentle and continuous, painterly throughout.
